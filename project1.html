Project Overview

Cloudy is a cloud storage and organization app with an emphasis on network collaboration and seamless integration.
Cloudy syncs with social networks and channels such as Facebook, WhatsApp, Slack and the liking. Users can store various file types, such as Google Drive files, Dropbox etc. Users can access items that are stored within a range of different apps, share them with multiple groups and online communities, and collaborate with their peers and colleagues in real-time.

The Client's Goal

Identify a ‘gap’ in the cloud storage market
Identify an audience whose cloud storage needs
are lacking the ideal tool

Research showed that the market is lacking an app that is:
Super intuitive to use
Consistent re: navigation and branding that ‘makes sense'

My Role

As the designer responsible for this client’s project, I was responsible for bringing life into this client’s product idea.

Through rigorous competitor research and user surveying, I came up with cloudy, a cloud storage and organization app.

Branding - cloudy comes to life

Ideation

It's not just about navigation. We needed to strap on our thinking caps and really ask ourselves:

  • How can we leverage the term ‘cloud storage’ and work with the imagery of clouds to represent our brand?
  • What does the name cloudy really mean?
  • How can we maximize the potential of visual communication?
  • We’re confined to the constraints of a limited drawing space, and a silent dialogue with our audience.

Look and Feel

Everything about the face of cloudy stems from where it all began, the design goal:
A product that is super intuitive to use and is consistent re: navigation and whose branding ‘makes sense’.

In order to achieve an intuitive, easy-to-use product, I modeled the majority of the UX and navigation inside
cloudy’s dashboards and screens off of Google Drive.
Since most cloudy users will have used Google Drive, they will be familiar with this layout, so the cloudy UX
should feel consistently natural, and fit them like a glove.

Brand Colors
The cloudy’s colors are blue and white, and within that several variations of blue. These represent the sky, a
natural habitat for any user. Blue and white are intuitive colors for any human.

In color psychology, writes 99designs (https://99designs.com/logo-design/psychology-of-color):
“Blue appears in over half of all logos because it represents intelligence, trustworthiness and maturity.
Technology companies and large corporations lean towards blue’s steadfastness and security. True blue will
make sure you’re taken seriously.”

If users will be backing up/storing their documents, photos, and files in general on cloudy, they will only do so
if they trust the product, and they believe their digital assets are secure. Blue is the best color to represent this
experience.

Logo
The logo, logically, is a friendly, cartoon-ish yet real looking illustration of a cloud. This represents modern
graphics, as cloudy is a modern SaaS product, but it also not too far fetched to what a cloud looks like in real
life.

Imagery
Overall, the imagery consists of modern, upbeat looking illustrations, representing cloudy’s young audience
and laid back, light feeling.

Typography
The font is Roboto, Google Material Design’s font because it is simple, clear, and looks great on both mobile and
desktop. And of course, it is familiar to any Google, Gmail, or Android user.


Changing in Branding Decisions

Colors
Originally, the idea was to use the three primary colors: Red, blue, and yellow. This would represent
the human’s most basic color scheme, and feel natural and intuitive to all.
However, as the branding process evolved, I saw that the design of logos and other imagery would
feel too heavy, and the colors wouldn’t blend well together to convey a light, intuitive feeling product.

Logo
The original logo and the final logo don’t look drastically different from eachother, as they are both
illustrations of a cloud, however the styling of each one is very different.
The first one looks more primitive, as it is a basic sketch of a cloud, which in theory was thought to
look more basic and natural to the user, but it didn’t actually convey the brand personality: a light,
airy vibe.

Imagery
At first I liked the idea of an umbrella to represent an ‘all-encompassing’ object that captures
everything you need. Yet again, although the literal connotation made sense,
it didn’t convey a sense of lightness and intutition.

Typography
While Lato looked nice and straightforward, similar to the style of Roboto, Lato seemed too ‘stickler’
and formal. Roboto is the typeface used in Google Material Design, and therefore a familiar, intuitive
choice.

Aesthetically, it has a less overwhelming and dominant presence on the screen.

Brand Identity

Finding the optimal styles for the Dashboard and Doc Editor

Aiming for cloudy’s functionality/navigation to use the same layout as Google Drive, I modeled the ‘Create’ and ‘Upload’ buttons off of the Google Drive Dashboard navigation.

However, my visual instinct was that they didn’t blend in to the Dashboard well, rather that they looked out of place.


The ‘design dilemma’ with this screen was about the color of the menu bar.
On the one hand, it would be logical for it to be the same color as the left-column navigation menu, since they are the two menus on the screen.

Yet logic aside, when looking vertically down the doc editor, the royal blue seems to interfere with the consistent navy coloring that’s used by the search bar, doc title, style editor, and doc input.

Conclusion

Designing cloudy from A to Z was a challenging yet rewarding process.
My first time designing a full fledged product, I saw that the more difficult decisions and choices you
make between design styles and directions, the more informed the final decisions are, and the more
confident you are about the product you’re delivering.

It was also interesting to aim to find a middle ground between creating similar interfaces to what your
target audience is used to interacting with, yet within that constraint, finding a way to differentiate the
design both visually and functionally.
